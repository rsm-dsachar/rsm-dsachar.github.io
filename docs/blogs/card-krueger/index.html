Title: “Replication of Card and Krueger (1994)” Subtitle: “Minimum Wages and Employment: A Case Study of the Fast-Food Industry” Author: “Devansh Sachar” Date: “2026-04-21”

Introduction

Card and Krueger (1994) examine the impact of minimum wage increases on employment in the fast-food industry. Their study focuses on a policy change in New Jersey, where the minimum wage increased, while neighboring Pennsylvania did not.

This creates a natural experiment that allows us to estimate the causal effect of minimum wage changes using a Difference-in-Differences (DiD) approach. The key idea is to compare how employment changes over time in New Jersey relative to Pennsylvania.

In this replication, we aim to reproduce the main finding of the paper: that the increase in minimum wage did not lead to a reduction in employment.

# Load data (FIXED PATH)
df = pd.read_fwf("../../data/public.dat", colspecs=colspecs, names=cols, dtype=str)

# Convert to numeric
for c in cols:
    df[c] = pd.to_numeric(df[c].replace(".", np.nan), errors="coerce")

# Construct employment
df["emptot"] = df["empft"] + 0.5 * df["emppt"] + df["nmgrs"]
df["emptot2"] = df["empft2"] + 0.5 * df["emppt2"] + df["nmgrs2"]

# Closed stores
df["closed"] = (df["status2"] == 3)
df["emptot2_table"] = df["emptot2"]
df.loc[df["closed"], "emptot2_table"] = 0

# Change in employment
df["demp"] = df["emptot2_table"] - df["emptot"]

# Treatment (NJ)
df["nj"] = df["state"]

# Balanced sample
balanced = df[df["emptot"].notna() & df["emptot2_table"].notna()].copy()

# Summary table
summary = (
    balanced
    .groupby("nj")[["emptot", "emptot2_table", "demp"]]
    .mean()
    .rename(index={0: "Pennsylvania", 1: "New Jersey"})
)

The difference-in-differences estimate is approximately 2.7–2.8, which closely matches the paper’s reported value (~2.76). This suggests that employment increased in New Jersey relative to Pennsylvania after the minimum wage increase.

Something Additional

The graph clearly shows the logic of the Difference-in-Differences approach. Employment decreases in Pennsylvania but slightly increases in New Jersey, creating a positive gap between trends.

Conclusion

This replication closely matches the findings of Card and Krueger (1994). The results suggest that the increase in minimum wage did not reduce employment in the fast-food sector.

More broadly, this demonstrates how Difference-in-Differences can be used to estimate causal effects in real-world policy settings.
