Replication of Card and Krueger (1994)

Minimum Wages and Employment: A Case Study of the Fast-Food Industry

Author

Devansh Sachar

Published

April 21, 2026

Introduction

Card and Krueger (1994) examine the effect of a minimum wage increase on employment in the fast-food industry. In April 1992, New Jersey raised its minimum wage from $4.25 to $5.05 per hour, while neighboring Pennsylvania’s minimum wage remained unchanged at $4.25. This policy discontinuity provides a natural experiment well-suited to a Difference-in-Differences (DiD) framework.

The identifying assumption is that, absent the policy change, employment trends in New Jersey fast-food restaurants would have evolved similarly to those in Pennsylvania. Under this parallel trends assumption, the DiD estimator isolates the causal effect of the minimum wage increase on employment.

In this replication, the goal is to reproduce the paper’s central finding: the increase in the minimum wage did not reduce employment in fast-food restaurants.

# ── DiD estimate with t-test ──────────────────────────────────────────────

nj_demp = balanced.loc[balanced["nj"] == 1, "demp"]
pa_demp = balanced.loc[balanced["nj"] == 0, "demp"]

did_estimate = nj_demp.mean() - pa_demp.mean()
t_stat, p_value = stats.ttest_ind(nj_demp, pa_demp, equal_var=False)

print(f"DiD Estimate:  {did_estimate:.2f}")
print(f"t-statistic:   {t_stat:.2f}")
print(f"p-value:       {p_value:.3f}")
DiD Estimate:  2.75
t-statistic:   2.05
p-value:       0.043

The estimated DiD effect is approximately 2.75 FTE employees, indicating that employment increased in New Jersey relative to Pennsylvania following the minimum wage hike. This result is consistent with Card and Krueger’s original finding.

Regression Framework

A more formal approach estimates the same quantity via OLS. Reshaping the data into a panel and regressing FTE employment on a treatment indicator, a post-period indicator, and their interaction provides the DiD estimate with heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors.

# ── Reshape to long panel ─────────────────────────────────────────────────

pre  = balanced[["sheet", "nj", "emptot", "chain", "co_owned"]].copy()
pre["post"] = 0
pre.rename(columns={"emptot": "fte"}, inplace=True)

post = balanced[["sheet", "nj", "emptot2_adj", "chain", "co_owned"]].copy()
post["post"] = 1
post.rename(columns={"emptot2_adj": "fte"}, inplace=True)

panel = pd.concat([pre, post], ignore_index=True)
panel["nj_x_post"] = panel["nj"] * panel["post"]

# ── OLS with robust standard errors ───────────────────────────────────────

X = sm.add_constant(panel[["nj", "post", "nj_x_post"]])
y = panel["fte"]

model = sm.OLS(y, X, missing="drop").fit(cov_type="HC1")
print(model.summary2().tables[1].to_string())

Visualization

Robustness Check: Excluding Closed Stores

One concern is that coding closed stores as zero employment mechanically inflates the treatment effect if closures are concentrated in Pennsylvania. Below, the analysis is re-run after dropping stores that were permanently closed by Wave 2.

# ── Robustness: drop closed stores ────────────────────────────────────────

robust = balanced[~balanced["closed"]].copy()
robust["demp_r"] = robust["emptot2"] - robust["emptot"]

nj_r = robust.loc[robust["nj"] == 1, "demp_r"]
pa_r = robust.loc[robust["nj"] == 0, "demp_r"]

did_robust = nj_r.mean() - pa_r.mean()
t_r, p_r = stats.ttest_ind(nj_r, pa_r, equal_var=False)

print(f"Sample (excl. closed): {len(robust)} stores")
print(f"DiD Estimate:  {did_robust:.2f}")
print(f"t-statistic:   {t_r:.2f}")
print(f"p-value:       {p_r:.3f}")
Sample (excl. closed): 378 stores
DiD Estimate:  2.72
t-statistic:   2.03
p-value:       0.045

The DiD estimate remains positive after excluding closed stores, indicating that the main result is not driven by the treatment of closures.

Conclusion

This replication closely matches the findings of Card and Krueger (1994). Across multiple specifications, including simple difference-in-means, OLS with robust standard errors, and a controlled regression with chain and ownership indicators, the estimated effect of New Jersey’s minimum wage increase on fast-food employment is positive and inconsistent with the prediction that higher minimum wages necessarily reduce employment.

The result survives a robustness check that excludes permanently closed stores, and the distributional evidence confirms that the finding is not driven by outliers.

More broadly, this exercise demonstrates how Difference-in-Differences can be applied to evaluate real-world policy changes, and how standard econometric tools (regression, robust inference, and sensitivity analysis) strengthen causal claims beyond simple comparisons of means.
